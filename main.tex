\section*{Abstract}

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%% Text %%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
This manuscript provides a consolidated, practitioner-oriented overview of widely used datasets and benchmarks for machine learning (ML) and computer vision (CV). It aims to serve as a compact entry point for selecting suitable benchmarks, understanding typical evaluation settings, and recognizing common dataset characteristics such as modality, scale, and annotation scope. Beyond listing canonical datasets, the manuscript highlights benchmark hubs and repositories that standardize access, splits, and metadata, and it emphasizes how dataset choice influences comparability, reproducibility, and the validity of conclusions. The overview is organized along two broad domains---ML and CV---and covers representative task families including tabular learning, time series, graphs, recommender systems, natural language processing, reinforcement learning, and multiple CV subareas (classification, detection, segmentation, 3D vision, remote sensing, vision--language, and medical imaging). The manuscript is intended as a living reference that supports both newcomers and experienced researchers in rapidly mapping the benchmark landscape and in making transparent, well-motivated dataset selections for experiments and reporting.

\section{Introduction and Motivation}

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%% Text %%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
Datasets and benchmarks are central to progress in machine learning and computer vision: they operationalize tasks, define evaluation protocols, and enable quantitative comparisons across methods. At the same time, dataset selection is a frequent source of hidden variance in experimental outcomes. Differences in label definitions, sampling strategies, train/validation/test splits, annotation noise, and domain coverage can easily outweigh architectural or optimization improvements. Consequently, responsible empirical work requires more than picking a popular dataset. It requires understanding what a benchmark measures, what it omits, and how its assumptions align with the intended deployment setting.

The benchmark landscape has expanded rapidly, spanning classical tabular problems, modern web-scale corpora for foundation models, specialized medical and scientific datasets, and increasingly multimodal benchmarks. In parallel, dataset access has shifted toward standardized repositories and loaders that facilitate reproducibility through versioning, checksums, and canonical splits. This manuscript consolidates representative datasets and benchmark suites across ML and CV into structured tables that summarize modality, approximate scale, and typical tasks. The goal is not exhaustiveness, but coverage of widely referenced anchors and practical starting points for experiment design, baselining, and reporting.

Throughout the manuscript, the term dataset refers to a curated collection of samples (e.g., images, time series, text, or graphs) with accompanying metadata and often annotations, while benchmark refers to a dataset paired with an evaluation protocol (metrics, splits, constraints, and reporting conventions). The presented overview is meant to be updated over time and to support transparent documentation practices, e.g., explicitly stating dataset versions, preprocessing steps, and evaluation protocols.

\section{Machine Learning}

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%% Text %%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
Machine learning benchmarks span a broad range of modalities and task formulations. Classical supervised learning on tabular data remains important for structured prediction in domains such as finance, marketing, and healthcare, while time series benchmarks capture forecasting and anomaly detection challenges under temporal dependencies and seasonality. Graph benchmarks support research on relational reasoning and representation learning for networks, and recommender-system datasets highlight issues such as sparsity, popularity bias, and temporal drift. In natural language processing (NLP), benchmark suites for understanding and question answering (QA) coexist with massive pretraining corpora that fuel modern large language models (LLM), making it important to distinguish evaluation benchmarks from pretraining data sources. Reinforcement learning (RL) benchmarks often come as environment suites or logged trajectories, and their evaluation depends strongly on interaction protocols (online vs. offline RL).

A recurring theme across ML benchmarks is the interplay between data scale, annotation quality, and evaluation stability. Small and well-curated datasets can support controlled comparisons but may saturate quickly or fail to reflect real-world variability. Large-scale datasets improve statistical power but increase the risk of dataset-specific shortcuts, spurious correlations, and untracked distribution shift. Moreover, some benchmarks are primarily historical (useful for continuity) while others are designed to stress robustness, fairness, or out-of-distribution generalization. For transparent reporting, it is good practice to document dataset versions, canonical splits, leakage checks, and any filtering or deduplication steps, especially when benchmarks are accessed via evolving repositories.

\section{Computer Vision}

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%% Text %%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
Computer vision benchmarks are typically organized by task families such as classification, object detection, segmentation, tracking, and 3D perception. While early datasets were relatively small and curated, modern CV research increasingly relies on large-scale, diverse datasets and on standardized evaluation protocols (e.g., mean average precision for detection, intersection-over-union for segmentation, or benchmark-specific leaderboards). The community also distinguishes between datasets intended for supervised evaluation and datasets primarily used for representation learning or pretraining. In addition, multimodal vision--language datasets have become prominent as foundation models blur the boundary between CV and NLP, introducing new concerns around web-scale collection, noise, and licensing constraints.

Choosing a CV benchmark requires attention to label taxonomies, annotation granularity (image-level labels vs. instance masks vs. panoptic labels), domain coverage (e.g., consumer photos, autonomous driving, or medical imaging), and the expected generalization regime. Many datasets embed biases---for instance, long-tail class frequency, geographic or demographic skew, or dataset-specific artifacts---that can lead to brittle improvements. Robustness-oriented benchmarks explicitly probe such failure modes via distribution shift, adversarial filtering, or domain transfer (e.g., renditions, sketches, or stylized images). For practical experimentation, it is also crucial to report preprocessing (e.g., image resolution and augmentation), evaluation details (e.g., IoU thresholds or per-class averaging), and any additional supervision (e.g., external pretraining), as these strongly affect comparability.

\section{Conclusion and Outlook}

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%% Text %%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
This manuscript consolidates a representative set of datasets and benchmarks for machine learning and computer vision, with an emphasis on practical descriptors (modality, approximate scale, and typical tasks) and on benchmark hubs that standardize access and evaluation. The resulting overview can support rapid benchmarking, baseline selection, and clearer reporting by providing a shared map of commonly referenced datasets across subfields ranging from tabular learning and time series to vision--language and medical imaging. Importantly, the tables should be interpreted as entry points rather than prescriptions. The most appropriate benchmark depends on the intended deployment context, the available supervision, and the desired generalization regime.

Looking ahead, several trends are likely to shape benchmark practice. First, evaluation will increasingly combine multiple axes (accuracy, calibration, fairness, robustness, efficiency, and energy/cost) rather than focusing on a single metric. Second, multimodal and foundation-model evaluation will require more careful separation of pretraining sources and downstream benchmarks, including explicit tracking of contamination and deduplication. Third, dataset documentation and governance (licensing, consent, provenance, and privacy constraints) will become more prominent, especially for web-scale and healthcare data. Finally, living benchmark repositories and community-maintained leaderboards will continue to evolve, making dataset versioning and reproducible pipelines essential for credible comparisons. This overview is therefore intended to be updated over time and to encourage transparent, well-documented benchmark selection and reporting.
